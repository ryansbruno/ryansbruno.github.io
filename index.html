Research

Moral Judgment & Harm Perception

Working Paper Target: Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied

Proportion Dominance in Subjective Harm Judgments

Ryan Bruno & [Co-authors]

How do people assess how much a victim has been harmed? Across three pre-registered studies (N = 1,811), we find that harm judgments are overwhelmingly driven by proportional loss rather than absolute magnitude. Stealing all of someone’s savings is judged as more harmful than stealing a larger absolute amount from a wealthier victim—even when absolute losses are equated. In Study 1 (within-subjects), Proportion Lost explained 76% of variance in harm judgments (η²p = .76), far exceeding Starting Wealth (η²p = .39). Study 3 shows that this bias extends to costly behavioral punishment with real monetary stakes. These findings challenge absolute-magnitude standards in legal sentencing and tort law.

Media Fairness & Consumer Perceptions

Working Paper Target: Journal of Consumer Research

Equal versus Proportional Media Representation of Two-Sided Issues

Ryan Bruno & [Co-authors]

When a news network covers a two-sided issue, should it give each side equal airtime or allocate coverage proportionally to public opinion? Across four pre-registered experiments (N > 1,000), consumers strongly prefer equal over proportional coverage (OR = 5.33), rate proportional coverage as more biased and less fair, and maintain this preference across six distinct coverage goals and 27 real contemporary issues. Self-interest moderates but does not reverse the preference. We ground these findings in distributive justice and procedural fairness theory, and discuss implications for journalism norms and the false balance critique.

Corporate Social Responsibility & Activism

Theoretical Paper

When Companies Take Sides: A Theoretical Framework for Corporate Activism

Ryan Bruno & [Co-authors]

Corporate activism—public positions by firms on contested social and political issues—has become a defining feature of contemporary business strategy. We develop a theoretical framework integrating CSR authenticity, stakeholder theory, and social identity to explain when company activism enhances versus undermines brand equity. We identify a critical distinction between brand-cause fit (genuine alignment between brand identity and the cause) and business-interest fit (strategic alignment with commercial interests), and propose that authenticity perceptions mediate the effect of activism on consumer outcomes.
